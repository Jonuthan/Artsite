Alpha und Omega für keine Grund

The Captive Wolf, Human Domestication, and The Web of Time

A wolf born in captivity does not know the wild. Raised within walls, in view of watchful eyes, it is shaped by structures older than itself. It learns the enclosure’s boundaries before discovering its own limits. Humans too. We are born enclosed within history, language, identity, expectation. Before we have any questions, we have a name. Before we can choose, we have inherited a role. Dasein ist geworfen. Geworfenheit—Thrownness The condition of existing within a world not of one’s making (Host Culture) Meaning, structure, and purpose are predetermined, arranged before one's arrival. Thrownness is not an event; it is the fundamental condition of being in the World and in Time. The captive wolf does not remember the wild; the domesticated human rarely questions the enclosure. We find ourselves within a structure and mistake it for Totality. No Alphas For years, people believed wolves lived according to strict dominance hierarchies, led by an "alpha" who ruled through force. But this structure only appeared in captivity. In the wild, wolves do not battle for rigid control—they live as fluid, cooperative families, not bound by strict ranks. The dominance struggle is artificial, an illusion created by confinement. So too with humans. Natural hierarchies, the belief in alphas and betas, rulers and subjects—these are the myths of a captive species. Structures seem absolute when they define the whole of one’s known world. But the fence is never the forest. https://fs.blog/chestertons-fence/ Stirner as the Omega Wolf In wolf myths, the omega is the lowest-ranking member, the outcast, the reject. Yet, in nature, the omega often becomes the wanderer—the wolf who leaves the pack, rejects imposed structures, and survives on its own terms. Stirner embodies this omega spirit. He does not strive to become an alpha, recognizing there is no true hierarchy to climb. He neither submits to power nor struggles for dominance. Instead, he steps beyond the fences, rejecting ownership by Structure — State, Morality, Ideology, or History. For Stirner, Geworfenheit does not mandate submission; being thrown into the world does not prevent choosing where one lands. The Web The captive wolf believes deeply in its enclosure — it fights within it, submits to it, and dreams of controlling it. But outside, there are no ranks, only movement. Consider the spider's web, invisible until it traps the fly. The fly becomes entangled, unaware of the web until too late. The spider waits patiently, feeding off what the web captures. Yet, both spider and fly are temporary; the web itself transient, vulnerable to disruption by a single sweep of a broom. You can choose: You can be the fly. You can be the spider. You can be the web. You can be the broom. Nothing remains fixed (Formless Form). "Geworfen, ja—aber nicht gebunden" Thrown, yes—but never bound. The enclosure remains only as real as your belief in it. "The spider’s web remains invisible until something becomes ensnared. Tradition, language, measurement — these invisible threads shape reality until consciousness reveals their presence. To become aware of the web is the first step toward wielding the broom; to sweep it aside, rearrange it, or weave it anew. To consciously choose your mythology is to master the very fabric from which reality itself is constructed." "We stand between paradoxes: We need cultural mythologies to provide context, yet consciousness demands we question and transcend those very mythologies. Tradition without consciousness is captivity; consciousness without tradition risks isolation and meaningless abstraction. The authentic self lives in dynamic tension — aware of tradition, but not enslaved to it."
